Assignment: MultiCriteria analysis (MCA)

Part 1: Explaining Multicriteria Analysis (MCA)

1. What is multicriteria analysis (MCA)?

Multi-Criteria Analysis (MCA) is a tool used to solve complex decision problems that involve multiple criteria, such as environmental impact, economic incentives, and social factors, which at times can be conflicting, and to combine them into a single, best possible outcome.

Figure 1: Flowchart showing the main MCA steps (Government Analysis Function)

2. Why is MCA valuable in GIS?

In real life, the projects tackled by environmental planners and managerial decisions are rarely straightforward and cannot be solved using isolated datasets. MCA allows scientists and decision makers to turn abstract ideas and criteria into spatial data layers. By overlaying these layers, the required trade-offs are easy to see and map out exactly where competing priorities can balance.

Figure 2: An example performance matrix (Government Analysis Function)

3. What types of questions can MCA help answer?

MCA is crucial for questions that do not have a single “good” answer, but rather multiple suitable solutions with different advantages and disadvantages. For instance, a question like:

"Where is the best place to build a new wind farm?"

require MCA to balance maximizing energy potential with minimizing construction costs while avoiding harm to wildlife habitats.

Part 2: MCA Problem Workflow Design

Project Goal: Finding the best location for a new offshore wind farm in the Dutch sector of the North Sea, while maximizing energy production, minimizing environmental impacts, and construction costs.

Criteria for Consideration:

  • Wind Speed
    Areas with higher average wind speeds, such as far-offshore deep-water zones or the northernmost open-sea sectors, are preferred for generating more energy and improving the efficiency of the wind farm.
  • Water Depth
    Moderately shallow waters are better in terms of turbine installation and maintenance costs.
  • Distance to Existing Power Infrastructure
    Locations closer to existing coastal grid connection points reduce transmission losses and infrastructure costs.
  • Environmental Protection
    Locations away from protected habitats and conservation zones are preferred to minimize impacts on biodiversity and comply with environmental regulations.
  • Fishing Activity
    Areas with lower fishing activity help avoid interfering with commercial fisheries and other industries in the marine zone
  • Distance from Shore
    Locations farther from the coastline are preferred because they reduce impacts on the well-being of coastal communities.

Required Data Layers:

  • Wind speed raster map to identify areas with the greatest energy-generation potential.
  • Water depth raster to determine suitable installation depths.
  • Marine protected area boundaries to exclude environmentally vulnerable regions.
  • Fishing activity map to minimize conflicts with commercial fishing.
  • Power infrastructure map showing offshore substations and coastal grid connection points.
  • Coastline layer to calculate distance from shore.

Workflow Implementation Steps

  • Data Preparation:
    Collecting, cleaning, and converting datasets into a common format and coordinate system, ready to use in a GIS.
  • Criteria Analysis:
    Analyzing important factors such as wind speed, water depth, and distance to power infrastructure and the coastline.
  • Applying Constraints:
    Removing protected areas, fishing sites, and locations with unsuitable water depths.
  • Reclassifying Criteria:
    Converting all criteria into a common suitability scale (e.g., 1–5).
  • Assigning Weights:
    Assigning a weight to each criterion based on its relative importance to the project.
  • Suitability Assessment:
    Combine all weighted criteria and constraints to produce a final suitability map showing the best locations for offshore wind farm development.
